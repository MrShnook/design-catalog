### Story

Bevellier is a family of condensed and simplified sans serif fonts.
The the most immediately-noticeable characteristics of the typeface
are its narrow letters at its rounded-off corners. Formal reduction
also plays a strong role in Bevellier’s design. Its simplicity is
visible in letters like the lowercase ’t’, where the crossbar does not
go across the letter’s entire vertical stroke. Bevellier’s letters are
also ‘spurless’; you can see this feature on the top-left corners of
letters like ‘m’, ‘n’, and ‘r’. Normally, the letters’ vertical
strokes would peak up above the left-hand sides of those letters’
arches, but the ‘spurs’ have been eliminated here. The Bevellier
family includes eight weights, which range from Extralight to Black.
Each weight has two fonts on offer: an upright and an italic. Just
like Bevellier’s upright fonts, Bevellier’s italics are spurless.
However, the italic letterforms are more symmetrical than the upright
letters. The parts of Bevellier’s upright letterforms that have
missing ‘spurs’ are angular; in the italics, they are curved. The
strokes of Bevellier’s letterforms all appear to be monolinear. but
that doesn’t mean Bevellier is without contrast. Thanks to the
family’s large number of weights – eight really is a lot – you can
combine two or more of them with each other to create headlines that
exhibit quite a bit of contrast! Bevellier’s lowercase letters have a
very tall x-height. The fonts’ short ascenders rise to the same height
as the tops of the capital letters. The fonts’ numerals are
proportional lining figures; in terms of size, they are lower than
Bevellier’s uppercase, but still taller than the lowercase letters.
Bevellier’s friendly and somewhat technical look makes it an ideal
choice for use in branding, editorial design, and poster design.
